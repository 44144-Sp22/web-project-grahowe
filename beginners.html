Picture yourself in the worst situation possible. The power is out, there's no cell phone service, and the internet is out indefinitely. What would you do? Who would you call? Where would you go?

The above scenario may never happen, but it's a good idea to believe that it will. As an amateur radio operator, you're ahead of the game. And in times of normalcy, ham radio can provide you with hours and hours of fun! There are so many activites to do as an amateur radio operator. Below are some examples of what you can do. Because amateur radio is evolving with new technology, the sky is the limit when it comes to activities!

Make friends and connections

Amateur radio can connect people who are miles apart from each other. You may have something in common! You may even form a lifelong friendship with someone on the other side, and you can even learn a new language. Operators are always willing to share tips and tricks, so don't forget to ask during your conversations about any advice. You may also get an elmer, or a person who acts as a mentor.

Talk to outer space

Astronaut Owen Garriot, W5LFL, was the first astronaut to bring amateur radio to space. Almost 40 years later, amateur radio remains prevalent in space exploration. On the Internationl Space Station, the astronauts aboard use various amateur radio equipment to talk to individuals and even students in schools across the globe. There are also several satellites no bigger than cell phones that are used to provide amateurs with scientific data. Below is an image from the ISS during a slow-scan television event. This is an amateur radio activity which allows individuals to receive images from the space station.

Build your own equipment

One of the best parts of amateur radio is building your own equipment. Amateur radio can help you learn about science, computers, electrionics, physics, and so much more! It can also help teach you the basics of electronics and soldering. If you like DIYing, ham radio is for you. There are no instructions when it comes to building your own equipment. All you really need is some basic knowledge, the right tools, and a creative imagination. So what will you build?

Contests and other activities

Many, if not all amateurs participate in activities within the hobby. These can include:

  • Fox Hunting: This is similar to a scavenger hunt. It is done using direction-finding techniques and specially-built antennas. If you've heard of Geocaching, Fox Hunting is quite similar.
  • Contesting: This activity requires you to make the most calls in a given time period, generally 24 hours. You receive points based on how many calls you log, and you can receive bonus points for different countries received, your operating power, and sometimes your mode of choice.
  • Organizational contests: Organizations such as the ARRL will host contests throughout the year. Examples of the ARRL's contests include RTTY Roundup, International Digital Contest, and even Kids Day, which is a day dedicated to the younger hams out on the air.
